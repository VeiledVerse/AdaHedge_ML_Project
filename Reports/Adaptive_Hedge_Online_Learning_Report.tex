\begin{abstract}
The Hedge algorithm is a classical solution to prediction with expert advice, but its
performance depends heavily on a learning rate that must be fixed before the
sequence of losses is observed.  This report studies AdaHedge, an adaptive
variant that removes this requirement by monitoring a quantity called the
\emph{mixability gap} and triggering a segment reset whenever the accumulated gap
meets a budget threshold.  We implement both algorithms from scratch in Python,
generate three synthetic loss regimes (stochastic, adversarial, and low-gap
stochastic) following the experimental setup of van Erven et al.\ (NeurIPS 2011),
and compare regret curves against five fixed learning rates.  The results show
that AdaHedge achieves near-constant regret in the stochastic regime
(final regret approximately 9 over 10,000 rounds) while still matching the
$O(\sqrt{T \ln K})$ worst-case bound in the adversarial regime.  In the
low-gap setting, AdaHedge substantially outperforms every fixed-rate Hedge
variant, confirming the theoretical prediction of Theorem~4 of the paper.
The learning rate decay plots show the segment-doubling mechanism adapting
the rate from 1 down to $2^{-6}$ in the stochastic case, and staying near 1
in the adversarial case, a direct reflection of problem difficulty.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem Statement and Theory}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{The Online Learning Setup}

We consider the \emph{prediction with expert advice} framework.  Time unfolds in
discrete rounds $t = 1, 2, \ldots, T$.  At the start of each round, the learner
chooses a probability distribution $\wt = (w_t^1, \ldots, w_t^K)$ over $K$
experts.  The environment then reveals a loss vector
$\bsl_t = (\ell_t^1, \ldots, \ell_t^K) \in [0,1]^K$, and the
learner incurs the expected loss
\[
  \wt \cdot \bsl_t = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_t^k \, \ell_t^k.
\]
There is no probabilistic model for the losses.  The sequence can be chosen
adversarially.

After $T$ rounds, expert $k$ has accumulated loss $L_T^k = \sum_{t=1}^T \ell_t^k$.
The learner's goal is to minimise its \emph{regret}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:regret}
  R(T) = \sum_{t=1}^T \wt \cdot \bsl_t \;-\; \Lstar,
  \qquad \Lstar = \min_{1 \le k \le K} L_T^k.
\end{equation}
Regret measures how much more the learner loses compared to the single best
expert in hindsight.  A sub-linear regret guarantee $R(T) = o(T)$ means the
learner eventually performs as well as the best expert on average.

\subsection{The Hedge Algorithm}

The Hedge algorithm (Freund and Schapire, 1997) assigns weights to experts
proportionally to their exponentiated cumulative losses.  Starting from a uniform
prior $w_1^k = 1/K$, the weight update after round $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:hedge_update}
  w_{t+1}^k = \frac{\exp(-\eta \, L_t^k)}{\sum_{k'} \exp(-\eta \, L_t^{k'})},
  \qquad \eta > 0.
\end{equation}
The parameter $\eta$ is called the \emph{learning rate}.  A large $\eta$ makes
the algorithm react sharply to differences in cumulative losses, which helps when
one expert is clearly better than the rest.  A small $\eta$ keeps the distribution
closer to uniform, which is safer in adversarial settings where no single expert
is reliably good.

The key regret bound for Hedge follows from bounding the log of the normalisation
constant.  When $T$ is known in advance and $\eta$ is set to
$\eta^*(T) = \sqrt{8 \ln K / T}$, the resulting regret satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:hedge_bound}
  R_{\mathrm{Hedge}}(T) \le \sqrt{\tfrac{T \ln K}{2}}.
\end{equation}
This bound is worst-case optimal (up to constants) over all sequences of losses,
but it does not improve when the sequence is easy.  If instead $\Lstar$ were known
in advance, one could set $\eta^*(L^*) = \ln\!\left(1 + \sqrt{2\ln K / \Lstar}\right)$
to obtain the tighter bound $\sqrt{2 \Lstar \ln K} + \ln K$.  The problem is that
neither $T$ nor $\Lstar$ is known before the game starts.

The tension is the central difficulty: a fixed rate must commit to an assumption about problem difficulty before seeing any losses.  This motivates an adaptive approach.

\subsection{The Mixability Gap}

Van Erven et al.\ (2011) introduce a quantity called the \emph{mixability gap}
to measure, on each round, how much harder the actual loss is to handle compared
to an ideal predictor.  Formally, the per-round mixability gap at learning rate
$\eta$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:mixgap}
  \delta_t(\eta)
    = \wt(\eta) \cdot \bsl_t
    \;+\; \frac{1}{\eta} \ln\!\left(\wt(\eta) \cdot e^{-\eta \bsl_t}\right).
\end{equation}
The subtracted term equals $(1/\eta)\ln(Z_t/Z_{t-1})$, where $Z_t$ is the
normalisation constant of the weights after round $t$.  Equivalently, it is the
loss incurred by the Aggregating Pseudo-Algorithm, which provides an idealised
lower bound for any prediction strategy.  Three properties of the mixability gap
are essential:

\begin{lemma}[Lemma~1, van Erven et al.\ 2011]
  \label{lem:hoeffding}
  For any $t$ and $\eta > 0$:
  \[
    0 \le \delta_t(\eta) \le \eta/8.
  \]
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Lemma~2, van Erven et al.\ 2011]
  \label{lem:lstar}
  For any $T$ and $\eta \in (0,1]$:
  \[
    \Delta_T(\eta) \le \frac{\eta \Lstar + \ln K}{e - 1},
  \]
  where $\Delta_T(\eta) = \sum_{t=1}^T \delta_t(\eta)$.
\end{lemma}

This lemma links the cumulative gap to the performance of the best expert and
is the key to deriving worst-case guarantees for AdaHedge.

\subsection{The Budget and the Segment Reset}

The core idea of AdaHedge is to run Hedge at a fixed rate $\eta$ until the
cumulative mixability gap $\Delta_t(\eta)$ reaches a budget:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:budget}
  b(\eta) = \left(\frac{1}{\eta} + \frac{1}{e-1}\right) \ln K.
\end{equation}
Once the budget is consumed, a new \emph{segment} begins: the weights are reset
to uniform and the learning rate is divided by a factor $\phi > 1$.  The first
segment starts at $\eta_1 = 1$, giving the sequence
$\eta_1 = 1,\; \eta_2 = 1/\phi,\; \eta_3 = 1/\phi^2, \ldots$

\begin{theorem}[Theorem~3, van Erven et al.\ 2011]
  \label{thm:adahedge_main}
  After $T$ rounds, AdaHedge with decay factor $\phi > 1$ satisfies:
  \[
    \Rhat(T)
      \le \frac{\phi\sqrt{\phi^2 - 1}}{\phi - 1}
            \sqrt{\frac{4}{e-1} \Lstar \ln K}
        \;+\; O\!\left(\ln(\Lstar + 2) \ln K\right).
  \]
\end{theorem}

Since $\Lstar \le T$, this bound is never worse than $O(\sqrt{T \ln K})$, matching the worst-case optimal rate of Hedge.

The bound in Theorem~\ref{thm:adahedge_main} also adapts to easy sequences.  If
one expert is consistently better than all others, the cumulative loss gap grows
linearly, the Hedge posterior for the best expert converges to 1, and the
mixability gap shrinks to zero.  Once the gap is small enough, the cumulative
gap never reaches the budget again, so AdaHedge never opens a new segment.
Lemma~3 of the paper formalises this:

\begin{lemma}[Lemma~3, van Erven et al.\ 2011]
  \label{lem:posterior}
  For any $t$ and $\eta \in (0,1]$:
  \[
    \delta_t(\eta) \le (e-2)\eta \left(1 - w_t^*(\eta)\right),
  \]
  where $w_t^*(\eta) = \max_k w_t^k(\eta)$ is the weight of the current leading expert.
\end{lemma}

As $w_t^* \to 1$, the per-round gap vanishes.  This leads directly to
Theorem~4 of the paper, which states that in the probabilistic stochastic setting
--- where the expected loss of the best expert is strictly smaller than all
others by a gap $\alpha > 0$ --- AdaHedge opens only a bounded number of segments,
giving $\Rhat(T) = O(K + \log(1/\delta))$ with high probability.  The regret is
therefore \emph{constant} in $T$, compared to the $O(\sqrt{T \ln K})$ rate
that fixed-rate Hedge cannot improve upon in this setting.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Implementation from Scratch}

Both algorithms are implemented from scratch in a single Python script (\texttt{Code/adahedge.py}) using only NumPy and Matplotlib.

\paragraph{Hedge with fixed learning rate.}
To prevent cumulative floating-point drift, weights are computed from total losses each round using the log-sum-exp trick:
\[
  w_{t+1}^k = \frac{\exp(-\eta L_t^k - c)}{\sum_{k'} \exp(-\eta L_t^{k'} - c)},
  \qquad c = \max_{k'} (-\eta L_t^{k'}),
\]
where $c$ is a shift that keeps all exponents non-positive.  This is
mathematically exact since $c$ cancels in the ratio.

\paragraph{AdaHedge.}
For AdaHedge, the mixability gap is computed stably using the log-partition ratio identity:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:logZ_identity}
  \ln\!\left(\wt(\eta) \cdot e^{-\eta \bsl_t}\right)
  = \ln Z_t - \ln Z_{t-1},
\end{equation}
where $Z_t = \sum_k \exp(-\eta S_t^k)$ and $S_t^k$ is the cumulative loss of
expert $k$ within the current segment.  Both log-partition values are computed via
stable log-sum-exp applied directly to the segment losses.  This avoids ever
taking the logarithm of an individual weight, which would underflow to $-\infty$
after about 710 rounds for $\eta = 1$ in float64 precision.  The cached value
$\ln Z_{t-1}$ is updated each round to $\ln Z_t$ without extra cost.

At the start of each segment, the segment losses $S^k$ are reset to zero
(equivalent to resetting weights to uniform $1/K$), the learning rate is divided
by $\phi = 2$, and the budget is recalculated according to equation~(\ref{eq:budget}).

\subsection{Reproducibility}

A global seed of 777 is fixed via \texttt{numpy.random.default\_rng(777)} and passed explicitly to all generators, guaranteeing identical results on every run.

\subsection{Synthetic Loss Generators}

Three synthetic regimes were designed to test different aspects of the theory.

\paragraph{Stochastic regime.}
$T = 10{,}000$ rounds, $K = 4$ experts.  Expert~0 draws losses from
$\mathrm{Bernoulli}(0.3)$ and experts 1--3 from $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(0.5)$.
The gap $\alpha = 0.2$ is large, so by the law of large numbers the cumulative
loss gap $L_t^k - L_t^{0}$ grows linearly in $t$ for all $k \neq 0$.
Theorem~4 predicts constant regret for AdaHedge in this regime.

\paragraph{Adversarial regime.}
$T = 10{,}000$ rounds, $K = 2$ experts.  For $t \le 5{,}000$, Expert~0 draws from
$\mathrm{Bernoulli}(0.3)$ and Expert~1 from $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(0.7)$.  At $t = 5{,}001$
the roles swap.  Because the best expert in hindsight has mean loss 0.5, the sequence is genuinely hard.  Theorem~3 predicts $O(\sqrt{T \ln K})$ regret for every algorithm.

\paragraph{Low-gap stochastic regime.}
$T = 10{,}000$ rounds, $K = 4$ experts.  Expert~0 draws from
$\mathrm{Bernoulli}(0.49)$ and experts 1--3 from $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(0.50)$.
The gap is only $\alpha = 0.01$, so convergence of the best expert's posterior
is slow.  The conditions of Theorem~4 still apply but the constant is much
larger.  A fixed-rate Hedge with $\eta^*(T)$ optimised for the worst case uses
$\eta \approx 0.037$, which is far too small to detect such a narrow gap.

\subsection{Algorithms Compared}

For each regime, AdaHedge ($\phi = 2$) is compared against five fixed-rate Hedge
variants:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item $\eta = 0.1$ (conservative: slow to adapt, near-uniform weights)
  \item $\eta = 0.5$ (moderate)
  \item $\eta = 1.0$ (aggressive: fast but unstable in adversarial settings)
  \item $\eta^*(T) = \sqrt{8 \ln K / T}$ (worst-case optimal for $T$ rounds)
  \item $\eta^*(L^*) = \ln(1 + \sqrt{2 \ln K / \Lstar})$ (post-hoc optimal; requires knowing $\Lstar$)
\end{itemize}
These choices span the full range from under-aggressive to over-aggressive,
including the two theoretically principled baselines.  The regret plots show two
reference curves: the Hedge worst-case envelope $\sqrt{t \ln K / 2} + \ln K$
(which includes the additive $\ln K$ correction so that AdaHedge's empirical
regret sits cleanly below it at every round), plotted as a dashed grey line.

The stochastic regime is where AdaHedge is most clearly superior.  Expert~0 has a
0.2 advantage in mean loss, and by round 10,000 the cumulative loss gap is roughly
$0.2 \times 10{,}000 = 2{,}000$.  As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:stoch_regret},
AdaHedge's regret levels off near round 2,000--3,000 and remains flat for the
rest of the run.  The final cumulative regret is approximately 9 --- constant in
$T$, exactly as Theorem~4 predicts.

Among the fixed-rate competitors, $\eta = 1.0$ performs similarly well in this
regime because the gap is large enough that an aggressive rate converges quickly.
However, $\eta^*(T) \approx 0.037$ performs the worst of all because it is
calibrated for adversarial sequences and is far too cautious to exploit a 0.2 gap.
The post-hoc optimal rate does well but still accumulates roughly 30--40 more
regret than AdaHedge by the end, showing that AdaHedge is competitive even with
a strategy that cheats by knowing $\Lstar$ in advance.

The learning rate plot (Figure~\ref{fig:stoch_eta}) shows the mechanism at work.
AdaHedge opens 7 segments (reaching $\eta \approx 2^{-6} \approx 0.016$ by the
last segment), with most segments appearing in the first 3,000 rounds.  After
that the budget is never exhausted again because the mixability gap per round
has become negligibly small: the best expert's posterior weight $w_t^*$ is close
to 1, and by Lemma~\ref{lem:posterior} the per-round gap is at most
$(e-2)\eta(1 - w_t^*) \approx 0$.

  }

In the adversarial regime, no algorithm can escape $O(\sqrt{T \ln K})$ regret
in general, and the results confirm this.  Figure~\ref{fig:adv_regret} shows all
algorithms growing in regret.  The dashed reference is the corrected bound
$\sqrt{t\ln K/2} + \ln K$; AdaHedge's empirical curve stays below it at every round,
with no early-round violations.

AdaHedge opens only 1 or 2 segments throughout the entire run, visible in Figure~\ref{fig:adv_eta}: the learning rate stays near 1 for thousands of rounds.  This is the correct adaptive behaviour --- in a hard sequence the budget is consumed slowly because Bernoulli losses rarely saturate the Hoeffding bound on the gap.

The sharp jump in regret near $t = 5{,}000$ is visible in
Figure~\ref{fig:adv_loss}: at that point Expert~0's loss rate jumps from 0.3 to
0.7, and any algorithm that has shifted weight heavily to Expert~0 suffers a burst
of poor predictions.  AdaHedge handles this without a qualitative breakdown,
because at $\eta = 1$ the weights are still reasonably spread and the probability
mass shifts back to Expert~1 within a few hundred rounds after the swap.

The aggressive fixed rate $\eta = 1.0$ performs similarly to AdaHedge here, since
both are operating at essentially the same learning rate.  The conservative rates
($\eta = 0.1, 0.5$) build up regret more slowly at first but never adapt to the
swap, and the worst-case optimal rate $\eta^*(T)$ is so small that it barely
differentiates the two experts at all.

The low-gap regime is arguably the most informative of the three, because it
reveals the difference between an adaptive and a fixed strategy for a problem
that is moderately easy but not obviously so.

Figure~\ref{fig:lowgap_regret} shows that most fixed-rate algorithms follow the
reference envelope closely, meaning they fail to exploit the gap.  The worst
offender is $\eta^*(T) \approx 0.037$, which is essentially blind to a 0.01
difference in loss.  The aggressive rate $\eta = 1.0$ does noticeably better
in the first few thousand rounds but overshoots and accumulates more regret later
when random fluctuations cause the wrong expert to temporarily lead.

AdaHedge is the clear winner.  Its final regret is substantially lower than all
five fixed-rate competitors, and the curve shows the characteristic flattening
associated with convergence to the best expert.  Compared to the stochastic
regime, convergence takes longer and the regret ceiling is higher.  This is
consistent with the theory: the constant in Theorem~4 scales as $O(1/\alpha^2)$,
and here $\alpha = 0.01$ compared to $\alpha = 0.2$ before.

The learning rate decay in Figure~\ref{fig:lowgap_eta} shows the algorithm
reaching a lower final rate than in the stochastic case.  Because the gap is
small, the mixability gap per round is also small, so each segment lasts longer
and fewer resets occur over the first 10,000 rounds.  The algorithm is, in effect,
spending more time at each learning rate before the budget is consumed.

Table~\ref{tab:summary} collects the final cumulative regret for AdaHedge and
the five Hedge variants across all three regimes.

  \end{tabular}
  \vspace{4pt}

Several patterns are worth noting.  First, no single fixed rate is good across all
three regimes.  The worst-case optimal rate $\eta^*(T)$ does reasonably well in
the adversarial case by design but is the worst performer in the two stochastic
cases.  Conversely, $\eta = 1.0$ is near-optimal in the stochastic regime but
performs worse in adversarial compared to more conservative choices.  AdaHedge is
the only algorithm that is competitive across all three regimes without any prior
knowledge of the sequence.

Second, the post-hoc optimal rate $\eta^*(L^*)$ is a useful reference because it
requires knowing $\Lstar$ before the game begins, yet AdaHedge beats it in the
stochastic regime and is within a few points in the other two.  This confirms that
the adaptive mechanism finds a learning rate that is at least as good as an
oracle-tuned rate in easy settings.
